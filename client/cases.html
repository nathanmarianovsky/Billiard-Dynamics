Magnetic Field Values of Interest
Notice if $B_i = 0$ (for $i = 1,2$) then $\mathbf{\ddot{x}} = 0$ and consequently the electron traverses along a straight line. On the other hand if we are dealing with $B_i \neq 0$ then by solving the system of differential equations above we come to see that under the influence of a magnetic field perpendicular to the plane an electron will traverse in circular trajectories. We also consider the possibility where $B_i = \infty$ so as to introduce a potential wall that the electron will never cross.
The Case of Classical Billiards
By setting $B_1 = 0$ and $B_2 = \infty$ we prevent the electron from crossing the boundary and ensure that it travels by straight lines in $\Omega$. More specifically, the non-crossing of the boundary limits to the electron bouncing off of the boundary according to the law of reflection.
The Case of Magnetic Billiards
By setting $B_1 \neq 0$ and $B_2 = \infty$ we prevent the electron from crossing the boundary and ensure that it travels by circles in $\Omega$. More specifically, the non-crossing of the boundary limits to the electron bouncing off of the boundary according to the law of reflection.
The Case of Inverse Magnetic Billiards
By setting $B_1 = 0$ and $B_2 \neq 0,\infty$ we prevent the electron from blowing up to infinity once it has crossed the boundary since it travels in circles outside of $\Omega$. Furthermore, in $\Omega$ the electron travels by straight lines.
The Case of Projective Billiards
By setting $B_2 = 0$ we ensure that the electron blows up to infinity, but at the same time we allow the electron to travel an infinite amount of time so as to come back from "the other side" when working with a projective space rather than the standard plane. If it happens to be that $B_1 = 0$ or $B_1 = \infty$ then the electron's trajectory will simply be a line or a line whose intersection with $\Omega$ is missing. Thus, the only interesting scenario will occur whenever $B_1 \neq 0,\infty$.
