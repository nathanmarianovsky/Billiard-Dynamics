Introduction
This website serves to provide vivid visualizations tied to the dynamics of billiards associated to an ellipse in the plane. Formally the goal is to study the trajectories an electron (or a billiard ball) traverses with different initial conditions (the position and velocity) and varying strengths of the magnetic field(s). By setting
$\Omega = \Big\{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \hspace{.1cm} \Big| \hspace{.1cm} \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} < 1 \Big\} \hspace{.3cm} \text{with} \hspace{.3cm} a,b \in \mathbb{R}$
and introducing the magnetic fields:
$\mathbf{B} = B_\Omega(\mathbf{x})\mathbf{\hat{k}} \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} B_\Omega(q) = \begin{cases} B_1, & \mathbf{x} \in \Omega \\ B_2, & \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \Omega \end{cases} \hspace{.3cm} \text{with} \hspace{.3cm} B_1, B_2 \in \mathbb{R}$
we can conclude under classical mechanics that with mass $m > 0$ and charge $q > 0$ (where the sign has already been incorporated) the electron must satisfy the following law of motion govered by the Lorentz force:
$\mathbf{\ddot{x}} = \begin{cases} \frac{qB_1}{m}\mathbb{J}\mathbf{\dot{x}}, & \mathbf{x} \in \Omega \\ \frac{qB_2}{m}\mathbb{J}\mathbf{\dot{x}}, & \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \Omega \end{cases} \hspace{.3cm} \text{where} \hspace{.3cm} \mathbb{J} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$
Most trivially we notice if $B_i = 0$ (for $i = 1,2$) then $\mathbf{\ddot{x}} = 0$ and consequently the electron traverses along a straight line. On the other hand if we are dealing with $B_i \neq 0$ then by solving the system of differential equations above we come to see that under the influence of a magnetic field perpendicular to the plane an electron will traverse in circular trajectories. Now if $B_1,B_2 < \infty$ the electron is allowed to enter and leave $\Omega$ by crossing the boundary, whereas the condition that $B_i = \infty$ imposes the restriction that the electron is not allowed to enter $\Omega$ or leave $\Omega$ if $i = 1$ or $i = 2$, respectively. With either region blocked off we instead allow the electron to bounce off of the boundary according to the law of reflection.
